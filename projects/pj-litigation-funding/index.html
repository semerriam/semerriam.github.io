In December, Match Group, the parent company of Tinder and Match.com, was sued by Tinder’s founder, Sean Rad, for $2 billion but only in discovery was it revealed that the case was in part financed by a litigation funder, Bench Walk Advisors. Lawyers for Match accused the other side of violating the NY Bar Association’s ethics rules and using litigation finance to pay off witnesses after communications between two witnesses, Sean Rad, lawyers for the plaintiff, and executives at Bench Walk came to light.

The small but rich industry of litigation funding, also sometimes called litigation finance or lawsuit lending, increasingly resides behind the country’s most lucrative legal cases— turning the judicial system into the latest investment opportunity for various players from loan lenders to private equity. Financiers make calculated decisions in paying legal costs for cases that they believe will ensure big payouts when the entity bringing the case to court wins their case.

47 of the industry’s largest funders manage $12.4 billion in assets, up 10% from 2020 according to an industry report. Only two funders are publicly traded companies so much of the industry’s financing is hidden from the public and the full size of the industry is difficult to determine.

In most courts, plaintiffs are not required to disclose where the funding for a case originates and the financing agreements typically include non-disclosure clauses, which limit what parties involved can reveal. Only on rare occasions, does a judge require disclosure or relationships revealed in discovery of a case. The industry’s lack of transparency or oversight allows for bad actors to take advantage and be shielded from public scrutiny.

In August of 2021, the U.S. Attorney General for the Southern district of New York charged a number of individuals with defrauding business and insurance companies by recruiting vulnerable individuals to stage trip-and-fall incidents and fraudulent lawsuits. The scheme was made possible by litigation funding, which paid for medical expenses and finances upfront for individuals but then reaped the rewards, when the plaintiffs were awarded their settlements.

According to the justice department’s press release, “the interest rates were so high that oftentimes the majority (if not all) of the proceeds that were awarded in the Fraudulent Lawsuits were paid to the Funding Companies.”

Litigation funding is becoming far more commonplace according to those working in the industry as larger law firms see the benefit in the financial risk being outsourced to these companies. Law firms are able to take on more expensive, longer-lasting, and riskier commercial cases like patent infringement and international arbitration as the cases (or portfolio of cases) are paid for by these outside private equity investors.

Like most private equity investing companies, litigation funders do not have to reveal where their funding comes from. Only if the company is investing more than $100 million does limited information have to be filed with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Out of the report’s top 47 funders, SEC records from ten companies provide a small glimpse of the industry.

When raising pools of capital, a number of funders rely on considerable foreign investment. Two pools of Brickell Key Asset Management— Brickell Key Investments LP and Juridica Investments Limited are reported as both having nearly all foreign investment and the latter registered in the tax haven, Guernsey.

Delta Capital Partners splits their Fund I into two pools, one registered in the Cayman Islands with 100 percent foreign investment and another with only U.S. investors. Their third pool, Delta JV I LLC requires a minimum investment of $100 million and is nearly all (99%) foreign investors.
